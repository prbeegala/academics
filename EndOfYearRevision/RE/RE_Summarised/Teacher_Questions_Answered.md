# ✡️ JUDAISM

## Slide 3 — Beliefs about G-d

### Why is G-d so important to Jews?
G-d is the **one and only creator** of the universe. Judaism is **monotheistic** — Jews believe everything that exists comes from G-d, so He is at the centre of their lives, worship and laws. The relationship with G-d is sealed by **covenants** (with Abraham and Moses), meaning Jews see themselves as G-d's chosen people, bound to follow Him.

### What is the Shema? What does it teach?
The **Shema** is the most important Jewish prayer, taken from **Deuteronomy 6**:
> *"Hear, O Israel: the Lord our G-d, the Lord is one."*

It teaches:
- There is **only one G-d** (monotheism).
- Jews must **love G-d** with all their heart, soul and strength.
- They must **teach these words to their children** and remember them at home and away.

The Shema is recited **twice a day** (morning and evening).

### What do Jews believe about G-d, and what physical symbols show this?

| Belief | What it means | Physical symbol |
|---|---|---|
| **Omnipotent** | All-powerful | — |
| **Omniscient** | All-knowing | — |
| **Omnibenevolent** | All-good | — |
| **Eternal** | Always existed, always will | The **Ner Tamid** (eternal light) in the synagogue |
| **One G-d (Shema)** | Monotheism | **Mezuzah** on doorposts containing the Shema |
| **Holy** — G-d's name is sacred | His name shouldn't be written or said carelessly | Writing **"G-d"** instead of "God"; saying *"Adonai"* / *"HaShem"* instead of the divine name |
| **Covenant people** | G-d chose the Jews and they chose G-d | **Tefillin** (small leather boxes with scripture worn during prayer); the **Tallit** (prayer shawl) |
| **Cannot be physically represented** | No idols allowed | The synagogue has **no statues or images** of G-d |

## Slide 4 — Moses

### Who was he?
Moses was the **most important prophet in Judaism**, born in Egypt while the Hebrews were enslaved. As a baby he was hidden in a basket on the Nile and raised in Pharaoh's palace. As an adult, G-d called him through the **burning bush** to free his people.

### What is a covenant?
A **covenant** is a sacred, binding **agreement between G-d and his people** — both sides make promises.

### What is the Covenant with Moses?
After the Exodus, on **Mount Sinai**, G-d gave Moses the **Torah and the Ten Commandments**. The covenant: G-d would protect and bless the Jews as His chosen people; in return, the Jews would **obey His laws**.

### What is the Exodus story?
- Pharaoh enslaved the Hebrews; Moses demanded *"Let my people go."*
- G-d sent **10 plagues** on Egypt; in the final plague (death of the firstborn), the Angel of Death **passed over** Jewish homes marked with lamb's blood.
- Pharaoh let them go; G-d **parted the Red Sea** so they could escape.
- After 40 years in the wilderness they reached the **Promised Land**.

### Why is the Exodus important for Jews?
- It shows G-d **rescues His people** and is faithful to His covenant.
- It is the **birth of the Jewish nation** as free people, not slaves.
- It is remembered every year at **Pesach (Passover)** through the Seder meal.
- It demonstrates G-d's power (plagues, parting the sea) and reinforces Jewish identity.

## Slide 5 — Jewish Law (Written and Oral)

### What is the Written Law? Where is it found?
The **Written Torah** = the **first 5 books of the Tanakh** (Hebrew Bible):
**G**enesis, **E**xodus, **L**eviticus, **N**umbers, **D**euteronomy.
It contains **613 mitzvot** (commandments) including the **Ten Commandments**.

### What is the Oral Law? Where is it found?
The **Oral Torah** = explanations and traditions of the written law, originally passed down **by rabbis** generation to generation. It was later written down as:
- **The Mishnah** (~200 CE) — summary of the oral law.
- **The Talmud** (~500 CE) — the Mishnah plus rabbinic commentary on it.

### What are the key points?
- The Torah covers **every area of life** — food (kosher), worship, festivals, family, justice.
- Includes the **Ten Commandments** received by Moses on Mount Sinai.
- **Kosher laws** (e.g. no pork, no shellfish, no mixing meat and dairy).
- **Shabbat laws** (no work from Friday sunset to Saturday sunset).

### Why is it important for Jews to follow?
- It is **G-d's direct instruction** for how to live.
- It is part of the **covenant** — keeping the law is the Jewish side of the deal.
- It **shapes Jewish identity** and keeps the community united across the world.
- Without the Oral Torah, the Written Torah's rules would be too brief to follow in everyday life — so both work together.

## Slide 6 — Worship

### Where do Jews worship?
At the **synagogue** (Jewish place of worship and community life) and at **home** (e.g. lighting Shabbat candles, the Pesach Seder).

### How do Jews worship? Key features
- **Prayer** three times a day in Orthodox Judaism (morning, afternoon, evening) from the **Siddur** (prayer book).
- The **Shema** is recited morning and evening.
- During prayer many men wear the **kippah** (skullcap = humility), **tallit** (prayer shawl) and **tefillin** (boxes of scripture on arm and forehead).
- Inside a synagogue:
  - **Ark (Aron Hakodesh)** — holds the Torah scrolls.
  - **Bimah** — raised platform where the Torah is read aloud.
  - **Ner Tamid** — eternal light, never extinguished, symbolising G-d's eternal presence.
  - **Menorah** and **Star of David**.
- In **Orthodox** synagogues, men and women sit separately; in **Reform** synagogues, together.
- **Shabbat** services on Friday night and Saturday morning.

### How does the way Jewish worship is organised point to what's important?
- The **Torah in the Ark** at the centre shows that **G-d's word is central** to Jewish life.
- The **Ner Tamid** burning always shows G-d is **eternal and always present**.
- The fact that prayer happens **together as a community** (a *minyan* of 10 is needed for some prayers) shows that **community and shared identity** matter as much as personal belief.
- **No images of G-d** anywhere — shows belief that G-d cannot be physically represented.
- Worship happens at **home too** — shows faith isn't separate from everyday life.

## Slide 7 — Yom Kippur

### Why is Yom Kippur important for Jews?
- It is the **holiest day of the Jewish year** — the **Day of Atonement**.
- It is the chance to **ask G-d for forgiveness** for sins committed in the past year and to **wipe the slate clean**.
- It encourages **teshuvah** (*return / repentance*) — turning back to G-d.
- Jews must also **seek forgiveness from anyone they have wronged** before G-d will forgive them, so it strengthens relationships in the community too.

### How is it commemorated?
- **25-hour fast** — no food or water from sunset to sunset (sundown the next day).
- **White clothing** is worn to symbolise **purity**.
- Long **synagogue services** of prayer and repentance.
- The **shofar** (ram's horn) is blown at the end to mark the close of the fast.
- No work, no washing, no leather shoes — focus is entirely on G-d.

## Slide 8 — Pesach (Passover)

### What is Pesach?
Pesach is the **7–8 day spring festival** that **remembers the Exodus from Egypt** under Moses. The name *Passover* comes from the Angel of Death "**passing over**" Jewish homes during the 10th plague.

### Most significant elements of Pesach

- **The Seder meal** on the first night — a special meal at home where the family **retells the Exodus story** using the **Haggadah** (the special Pesach book).
- **The Seder plate** with symbolic foods:

  | Food | Meaning |
  |---|---|
  | **Matzah** (unleavened bread) | Hebrews left Egypt so fast their bread didn't have time to rise |
  | **Maror** (bitter herbs) | Bitterness of slavery |
  | **Charoset** (sweet apple/nut paste) | The mortar slaves used to build for the Egyptians |
  | **Karpas** (parsley, dipped in salt water) | Spring; tears of slaves |
  | **Z'roa** (lamb shank) | The Passover lamb sacrifice |
  | **Beitzah** (egg) | Mourning + cycle of life |

- **No leavened bread (chametz)** for the whole festival — homes are thoroughly cleaned to remove every crumb.
- **Four cups of wine** drunk during the Seder, each representing one of G-d's promises in Exodus.
- A cup is set aside for the **prophet Elijah**.
- The youngest child asks the **Four Questions** ("Why is this night different…?") to start the retelling.

# ✝️ CHRISTIANITY

## Slide 10 — The Trinity

### What is it?
The Christian belief that there is **one God in three persons**: the **Father**, the **Son** (Jesus) and the **Holy Spirit**. Three persons, **one God** — not three gods.

### Significance of each person

| Person | Role / significance |
|---|---|
| **Father** | The **creator** of the universe; the loving G-d of the Old Testament. |
| **Son** | **Jesus Christ** — God made human (the *Incarnation*); he died and rose again to bring **salvation**. |
| **Holy Spirit** | God's **active presence** in the world today — guiding, comforting and inspiring believers. Came at **Pentecost**. |

### Why is the Trinity controversial?
- It seems to **contradict monotheism** (the belief in one God). Jews and Muslims reject the Trinity because they say it makes Christians look like they worship three gods.
- It is described as a **mystery** that human minds cannot fully understand — many Christians have struggled to explain it without slipping into heresy.
- Historically it caused **major splits** in the Church (e.g. the **Arian controversy** — was Jesus fully God or a created being?). The doctrine had to be defined at the **Council of Nicaea (325 AD)**.
- The dispute over the *Filioque* clause ("and the Son" — about the Holy Spirit's origin) was one cause of the **Great Schism in 1054**.

## Slide 12 — Sin, Sacrifice, Salvation

### Sin — what does it refer to?
**Sin** is any thought or action that **separates humans from G-d**, breaking His laws. Christians believe in **original sin** — that all humans are born with a tendency to disobey G-d, because of **Adam and Eve** eating the forbidden fruit in the Garden of Eden (Genesis 3).

### Sacrifice — whose?
**Jesus' sacrifice on the cross.** In the Old Testament, Jews sacrificed animals (lambs, goats) to atone for sin. Christians believe **Jesus is the "Lamb of God"** — his death is the **perfect, final sacrifice** that takes away the sins of all humanity (the *scapegoat*: one being takes on the sins of many).

### Salvation — what does it refer to?
**Salvation** = being **saved from sin and death**, with the promise of **eternal life in heaven**. Christians believe salvation is only possible **through Jesus' death and resurrection**.

### Why is this so significant for Christians?
- It explains **why Jesus had to die** — not as a tragedy, but as a deliberate act of love that fixes the broken relationship between humans and G-d.
- It gives Christians **hope of eternal life** despite their sins.
- It means salvation is a **free gift of grace**, not something humans can earn alone.
- It shapes Christian worship — the **Eucharist (Holy Communion)** re-enacts Jesus' sacrifice every week.
- Without sin, sacrifice and salvation, **there is no Christianity** — it is the core of the whole faith.

## Slide 13 — The Resurrection

### When was it?
**Easter Sunday**, three days after Jesus' crucifixion on Good Friday (c. 30–33 AD, in Jerusalem).

### What happened?
- Jesus was buried in the tomb of **Joseph of Arimathea**, with a stone rolled across.
- At **dawn on Sunday**, **Mary Magdalene** and other women went to anoint the body.
- An **earthquake**; an **angel** rolled the stone away; guards collapsed in fear.
- The tomb was **empty**. The angel said: *"He is not here; he has risen."*
- Jesus then **appeared** to Mary, then the disciples, then **doubting Thomas** (who touched his wounds), then to over 500 people, and finally to **Paul** on the road to Damascus.

### Why is this event significant for Christians?
- It **proves Jesus is God** — only G-d can defeat death.
- It **validates Jesus' teachings** — if he rose, what he said must be true.
- It **conquers sin and death** for humanity — Christians can also hope for eternal life.
- St Paul: *"If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile."* (1 Corinthians 15)
- It transformed the **disciples** — they went from terrified deserters to bold preachers willing to die as martyrs.

### How does it fit into Holy Week?
- The Resurrection is the **climax** of Holy Week — the joyful end after Good Friday's death.
- It completes the pattern of **sin → sacrifice → salvation**: Jesus dies for human sin, and his resurrection is the proof that the sacrifice worked.

### How is it commemorated today?
- **Easter Sunday** services — the most important service of the Christian year.
- **Easter Vigil** on Saturday night with a paschal candle.
- **Singing of "Alleluia"** (silenced during Lent) returns.
- **Easter eggs** symbolise the empty tomb and new life.
- The whole **Easter season** lasts 50 days until **Pentecost**.
- Every Sunday is a "mini-Easter" celebrating the resurrection.

## Slide 14 — Constantine's conversion

### Who was Constantine?
**Constantine the Great** was a **Roman Emperor** (ruled 306–337 AD). Before him, Christianity had been a **persecuted, illegal minority religion** for nearly 300 years.

### Why did he convert to Christianity?
Before the **Battle of the Milvian Bridge (312 AD)** against his rival Maxentius, Constantine had a **vision of a cross in the sky** with the words *"In this sign you will conquer."* He had his soldiers paint a Christian symbol (the **Chi-Rho**) on their shields, won the battle, and credited the Christian G-d for his victory.

### What happened to Christianity as a consequence?
- **313 AD — Edict of Milan**: Christianity became **legal**; persecution ended; confiscated property returned to Christians.
- Christians could **worship openly**, build churches and own property.
- Constantine funded **massive church-building** (e.g. Old St Peter's Basilica in Rome).
- **325 AD — Council of Nicaea**: Constantine called bishops together to define official Christian doctrine — produced the **Nicene Creed**.
- Christianity went from being a **persecuted faith of slaves and outcasts** to the **religion of the Roman Empire** (by 380 AD under Theodosius it became the *official* state religion).

### On balance, was Constantine's conversion beneficial or detrimental?

**Beneficial:**
- Ended brutal persecution that had killed thousands.
- Allowed Christianity to **spread rapidly** across the Empire.
- Gave the Church **resources** to build, organise and unify doctrine (Nicaea).
- Preserved Christian texts and traditions that might otherwise have been lost.

**Detrimental:**
- The Church became **politically powerful and wealthy** — some say it lost its original spirit of humble service.
- Christianity became tangled up with **state power** and military force (very different from Jesus' message of peace).
- Hypocrisy: some people converted **for political/career reasons**, not genuine belief.
- Set up centuries of **Church–state conflict** that would later cause splits (Great Schism, Reformation).

**Balanced view:** *On balance, most historians say beneficial — without Constantine, Christianity might have remained a small persecuted sect. But the cost was that the Church became as much a political power as a spiritual movement.*

## Slide 15 — Pick TWO key events

> Your teacher wants you to **pick two** of these four. Quick summaries of all four are here; the two you know best are highlighted ⭐.

### ⭐ A) St Peter and St Paul

**St Peter:**
- Real name **Simon**, a fisherman from Galilee. Jesus renamed him **Petros** (Greek for "rock"): *"On this rock I will build my Church."*
- One of Jesus' **closest disciples**.
- **Denied Jesus three times** on the night of his arrest, but was forgiven.
- After the resurrection became leader of the early church in **Jerusalem**.
- **Acts 10 — Peter and Cornelius:** had a vision that opened Christianity to **Gentiles** (non-Jews) — they didn't need to become Jews first.
- Died a **martyr** in Rome (crucified upside-down by Nero, by tradition).
- **Catholics see him as the first Pope.**

**St Paul:**
- Originally **Saul of Tarsus**, a strict Pharisee who **persecuted Christians**.
- On the **road to Damascus** (~35 AD) he had a vision of the risen Jesus and was struck blind; he converted and became Paul.
- Made **three missionary journeys** across the Roman Empire — Syria, Greece, Turkey, Rome — spreading the gospel.
- Wrote **13 of the 27 books of the New Testament** (the Epistles/letters).
- Faced **beatings, prison, shipwrecks** for his faith; eventually executed in Rome.

**Why significant?**
- Peter = **continuity and authority** — the rock of the Church, link back to Jesus, first Pope.
- Paul = **expansion** — opened the faith to the wider non-Jewish world and shaped Christian theology (e.g. *justification by faith*, *original sin*).
- Together they turned Jesus' small Jewish movement into a **worldwide religion**.

### ⭐ B) Roman Persecution

**Why were Christians persecuted?**
- They refused to **worship Roman gods** or pray to the emperor as a god.
- Romans believed disrespect for the gods would bring disaster (plague, famine, defeat) — Christians had to be **propitiated** = punished to keep the gods happy.
- Jews had a legal **exemption** from emperor worship; Christians did not.
- Made an easy **scapegoat** when things went wrong.

**Three case studies:**

| Martyr | Year | What happened |
|---|---|---|
| **Christians under Nero** | 64 AD | After the **Great Fire of Rome**, Nero blamed Christians. They were burned alive as torches in his garden, crucified, fed to dogs. *(Source: Tacitus)* |
| **Polycarp** | c. 155–160 AD | Bishop of Smyrna, aged **86**. Refused to deny Christ: *"For 86 years I have been His servant, and He has done me no wrong."* **Burned alive**. |
| **Vibia Perpetua** | 203 AD | Young noblewoman in Carthage, nursing a baby. Refused to recant even when her father begged. Killed in the arena. *"I am a Christian."* |

**What happened to the religion as a consequence?**
- Far from destroying it, persecution **strengthened** Christianity:
  - *"The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church"* (Tertullian).
  - Martyrs **inspired** others to convert.
  - Created strong **community bonds** between persecuted believers.
- Christians cared for the poor and the sick (e.g. during plagues when pagans fled), which won admiration.
- The faith spread along **Roman trade routes** and roads.
- Set up the conditions for **Constantine's conversion (312 AD)** and legalisation.

### C) The Great Schism (1054 AD) — Quick summary

The **split** between the **Roman Catholic Church (West)** and the **Eastern Orthodox Church (East)**.

| Cause | Type | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Filioque clause** | Doctrine | West added "and the Son" to the Creed; East refused. |
| **Papal authority** | Power | West said the Pope ruled all Christians; East said the 5 patriarchs were equal. |
| **Language** | Practical | West used Latin; East used Greek. |
| **Bread** | Practical | West: unleavened (Last Supper); East: leavened (life/resurrection). |
| **Constantinople vs Rome** | Power | East said Constantinople was equal to Rome. |

In 1054, Cardinal Humbert excommunicated the Orthodox patriarch in Constantinople, who excommunicated him back. The result was **two separate churches** that have stayed split to this day.

### D) The Reformation (1517–) — Quick summary

- **Martin Luther** (German monk) nailed his **95 Theses** to the church door at Wittenberg in **1517**, attacking **indulgences** (paying money to reduce time in purgatory).
- Other reformers followed: **Calvin** (Switzerland) and **Henry VIII** (England — Church of England, 1534).
- **Consequence:** the Western Church split into **Catholic** and **Protestant** branches. Bible translated into local languages (e.g. **Luther's German Bible**); decline of papal power; wars of religion across Europe.

## Slide 18 — Rightly Guided Caliphs

### Who were they?
After Muhammad's death (632), **four close companions** led the Muslim community. Sunnis call them the **Rashidun** (*"Rightly Guided"*).

| # | Caliph | Years | Known for |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Abu Bakr** | 632–634 | Muhammad's best friend; reunited the Arabian tribes; **began compiling the Qur'an**. |
| 2 | **Umar** (ibn al-Khattab) | 634–644 | **Massive expansion** of Islam (Persia, Egypt, Syria, Jerusalem). Strong and **just administrator**. |
| 3 | **Uthman** (ibn Affan) | 644–656 | Produced the **standard written Qur'an** still used today. Assassinated. |
| 4 | **Ali** (ibn Abi Talib) | 656–661 | Muhammad's **cousin and son-in-law**. **Shi'a Muslims** believe he should have been the first caliph — origin of the **Sunni–Shi'a split**. |

> 🧠 Mnemonic: **A**bu Bakr, **U**mar, **U**thman, **A**li → **"AUUA"**

### Why are they important?
- They **preserved Muhammad's mission** after his death — the community could have fallen apart.
- They **expanded Islam** from a small Arabian community to a vast empire (Spain → Persia within 100 years).
- **Uthman's standardised Qur'an** means Muslims everywhere read the **same text** in **the same Arabic** today — incredible unity.
- The dispute over **Ali's leadership** is the origin of the **Sunni / Shi'a split**, which still defines Islam today.
- For Sunnis, the Rashidun period is a **golden age** — a model of just, faithful leadership.

## Slide 19 — The Qur'an and Hadiths

### What is the Qur'an?
The **Qur'an** is the **literal word of Allah** as revealed to Muhammad through the angel **Gabriel** over **23 years** (610–632 CE). It has **114 surahs (chapters)** and was revealed in **Arabic**.

### How is it used?
- **Read in Arabic** during the **five daily prayers (salat)**.
- The whole Qur'an is read across the **month of Ramadan**.
- Muslims **memorise** it — someone who knows the whole Qur'an by heart is called a **Hafiz** (high honour).
- It is the **highest source of authority** in Islamic law and ethics.
- Treated with great respect — washed hands before touching, never placed on the floor, often on the highest shelf.

### Why is the Qur'an important?
- It is **Allah's direct, unchanged word** — Muslims believe it has been preserved perfectly since Uthman standardised it.
- It contains **guidance on every part of life** — worship, family, business, justice.
- It **unites Muslims worldwide** — everyone reads the same Arabic text.
- It is considered the **greatest miracle** given to Muhammad.

### What are the Hadiths?
The **Hadiths** are **reports of what Muhammad said, did, or approved of**, collected by Muslim scholars after his death. Together with his actions, they make up the **Sunnah** (Muhammad's example).

### How are they used?
- To **explain how to live by the Qur'an** in everyday life — the Qur'an may say *pray*, but the Hadiths show **how**, **when** and in what direction.
- Used in **Islamic law (Sharia)** as the **second source** of guidance after the Qur'an.
- Scholars **rank Hadiths** by reliability (*sahih* = strongest; *daif* = weak).

### Why are the Hadiths important?
- The Qur'an alone wouldn't tell Muslims **how to put its teachings into practice** — the Hadiths fill that gap.
- They let Muslims **follow Muhammad's example** directly — his life is the model for a perfect Muslim life.
- They are essential to **Sharia law** and to questions the Qur'an doesn't cover directly.

> ⚖️ **Difference in one line:** Qur'an = **Allah's words**; Hadith = **Muhammad's words and actions**. Both are authoritative, but the Qur'an comes first.

## Essay A — *"A synagogue is not essential for Jewish communities."* Discuss. (12)

**FOR (the statement — a synagogue isn't essential):**
- G-d is **omnipotent and omnipresent**, so He can be worshipped anywhere.
- After the **Temple was destroyed (70 CE)**, Jews still kept their faith without a central building.
- Many key Jewish events happen **at home** — Shabbat candles, Pesach Seder, kosher food, mezuzah.
- Personal prayer can happen anywhere — Jews don't need a priest.

**AGAINST (a synagogue IS essential):**
- It is the **centre of community worship** — a *minyan* of 10 men is needed for some prayers.
- It is the **centre of learning** — Torah classes, bar/bat mitzvah preparation, children's education.
- Major festivals like **Yom Kippur** require the long synagogue services and the blowing of the **shofar**.
- The **Ark, Bimah and Ner Tamid** are powerful symbols that anchor Jewish identity.

**Justified conclusion (example):**
> In conclusion, I disagree with the statement. Although Jews can worship G-d anywhere and their faith has survived without a temple, the synagogue is still very important because it brings the community together and helps maintain traditions across generations. Overall, while it may not be completely essential, it plays such a key role in worship, learning and community life that it is very difficult to replace.

## Essay B — *"The Resurrection is the most important event in Christianity."* Discuss. (12)

**FOR:**
- It **proves Jesus is God** — only G-d can defeat death.
- It **completes salvation**: without it, Jesus' death would just be a tragedy. Paul: *"If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile."*
- It transformed the **disciples** — Peter went from denying Jesus three times to dying as a martyr.
- It is the foundation of every Sunday service and the heart of Easter.

**AGAINST (other events matter just as much):**
- The **crucifixion** is the actual moment of sacrifice that pays for sin — without it, no resurrection.
- The **Incarnation** (Jesus becoming human) and the **Last Supper** founded the Eucharist.
- **Pentecost** sent the Holy Spirit — without it, the Church couldn't have begun.
- The **Sermon on the Mount** gave the moral teaching that defines Christian life.

**Justified conclusion (example):**
> I agree that the Resurrection is the most important event in Christianity. Although the crucifixion makes the sacrifice for sin possible, it is the Resurrection that proves Jesus is G-d and that the sacrifice worked — without it, Christianity simply collapses, as Paul says. The other events matter, but the Resurrection is the one that gives Christians their hope of eternal life.

## Essay C — *"The Qur'an is more important than the Hadiths."* Discuss. (12)

**FOR:**
- The Qur'an is the **literal word of Allah**; Hadiths are only Muhammad's words.
- Used in **every prayer** five times a day, in Arabic.
- It is **perfectly preserved** since Uthman's compilation; Hadiths vary in reliability.
- Memorising it is one of the highest acts of devotion.

**AGAINST:**
- Without the Hadiths, Muslims wouldn't know **how to actually live** by the Qur'an — e.g. how to perform salat, how to fast in Ramadan.
- Hadiths preserve **Muhammad's example (Sunnah)**, and Muhammad is the perfect model for Muslim life.
- **Sharia law** depends on both — neither alone is enough.
- For Shi'a Muslims, Muhammad's family and example carry huge weight.

**Justified conclusion (example):**
> Overall, I agree the Qur'an is more important because it is Allah's direct word, not a record of a human prophet. But this does not mean the Hadiths are unimportant — without them, the Qur'an would be hard to put into daily practice. The two work together: the Qur'an comes first, but the Hadiths are essential to applying it.
