### 🔑 Key Ideas

- A **parallel circuit** has **two or more branches** between the same two points.
- Current has **more than one path** to choose.
- **Voltage is the SAME across each branch** (every branch gets the full source voltage).
- **Current splits at junctions** and recombines at the next junction. Branch currents **add to the total**: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ + …
- Each branch's current depends on its own resistance: I_branch = V ÷ R_branch.
- Adding more branches **decreases** the total resistance (more paths = easier flow). Total R is always **less than the smallest single resistor**.
- **Branches are independent**: if one branch breaks, the others still work.
- That's why **houses are wired in parallel** — every appliance gets 230 V and they can be switched on/off independently.
- For identical bulbs in parallel, **each bulb is as bright as a single bulb on its own**, because each gets the full cell voltage.

---

### ✏️ Worked Example

**Q.** A 6 V battery is connected to two resistors **in parallel**: R₁ = 2 Ω and R₂ = 3 Ω.
Find: (a) voltage across each branch, (b) current in each branch, (c) total current from the battery.

**A.**
(a) Voltage across each branch = **6 V** (same as the battery).
(b) I₁ = V ÷ R₁ = 6 ÷ 2 = **3 A** ; I₂ = V ÷ R₂ = 6 ÷ 3 = **2 A**.
(c) I_total = I₁ + I₂ = 3 + 2 = **5 A**.
