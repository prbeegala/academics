### 🔑 Key Ideas

- A **series circuit** has only **one loop** — one path for the current.
- **Current is the same** at every point in a series circuit. Move the ammeter anywhere — same reading.
- **Voltage shares** between the components. The voltages across the components **add up to the cell's voltage**: V_cell = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + …
- A **bigger resistance** gets a **bigger share** of the voltage.
- **Total resistance adds up**: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …
- More resistors in series → **less current**.
- If **one component breaks**, the loop opens and **all components stop working**.
- **Cells in series add up** in voltage (e.g. two 1.5 V cells in series = 3 V).
- Use **V = I × R** (Ohm's law) for calculations.

---

### ✏️ Worked Example

**Q.** A 9 V battery is connected in series with two resistors: R₁ = 1 Ω and R₂ = 2 Ω.
Find: (a) total resistance, (b) current in the circuit, (c) voltage across each resistor.

**A.**
(a) R_total = 1 + 2 = **3 Ω**
(b) I = V ÷ R = 9 ÷ 3 = **3 A**
(c) V₁ = I × R₁ = 3 × 1 = **3 V**; V₂ = I × R₂ = 3 × 2 = **6 V**
Check: 3 V + 6 V = 9 V ✓ (matches the battery)
