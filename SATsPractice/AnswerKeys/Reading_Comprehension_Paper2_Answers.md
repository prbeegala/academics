# Answer Key — Reading Comprehension Paper 2 (*The Boy Who Watched the Sky*)

1. **On his grandmother's roof terrace in Jaipur** *(accept "on the roof" / "on the roof in Jaipur")*. (1)

2. **ancient** *(also accept "slow")*. (1)

3. **He already knows the night sky very well.** (1)

4. Any clear point + evidence, e.g.: (2)
   - She understands he is **wonderstruck** — his eyes are "shining" — and doesn't want to spoil the moment.
   - She remembers being taught the stars by her **own grandmother**, so she values this kind of moment.
   - She brings him **warm spiced milk and sits with him**, which shows she shares his wonder rather than disapproving.
   *(1 mark for a sensible point, 1 mark for evidence.)*

5. The stars and sky have been there far longer than any human writing, so they are the oldest "book" / story we have. (1) Unlike a house or money, no one can steal the sky from you — it belongs to everyone and is always available. (1)

6. a) **A simile.** (1)
   b) It makes the comet sound **friendly, gentle and special** — like a visitor from far away, not something frightening. Suggests it has travelled an enormous distance and is welcome. (1)

7. **"his eyes shining"** *(accept "his eyes were shining")*. (1)

8. Any **two** of: (2)
   - He is now called **Dr Arun Mehra** (a title used by scientists).
   - He works in an **office in California** (no longer at home in India).
   - He uses an **orbiting telescope** / looks at **data and pixels on a screen** (modern scientific tools).
   - He has **colleagues** / a **team** that built the telescope.
   - He is studying something **out beyond Neptune** (proper astronomy work).

9. Because it shows a **small boy on a rooftop pointing at the sky** with a cup of milk — it looks sweet/childish, very different from the adult scientist at his desk. (1)

10. Award up to 3 marks for points such as: (3)
    - Real discoveries often start with **curiosity, not equipment** — a child looking up can lead to a lifetime of science.
    - The writer wants us to feel that **anyone, anywhere can begin a discovery** — you don't need a laboratory to start.
    - The phrase **"look up instead of looking away"** suggests discovery is a choice to **pay attention** when others wouldn't.
    - It links Arun the boy and Arun the scientist — his career grew from that one rooftop night.

11. Correct order: (2)
    - **2** Arun and Nani drink warm spiced milk together.
    - **1** Arun first notices a smudge of silver light in the sky.
    - **5** Arun reaches for his phone to call someone.
    - **4** Three pixels on a screen form a careful pattern.
    - **3** Nani tells Arun about her own grandmother.
    *(2 marks all correct; 1 mark if 3 or 4 are correct.)*

12. Any **two** of: (2)
    - Both halves are about Arun **seeing something new in the sky** (a comet / unknown object beyond Neptune).
    - Both happen at **night / in quiet places**.
    - The **photograph on his desk** is a direct echo of the opening rooftop scene.
    - The story begins and ends with Arun **looking up / making a discovery**.
    - At the start his Nani is with him; at the end he wants to **call her** — she frames the whole story.

13. **Nani.** (1) Evidence: the photograph on his desk is of his rooftop night with her; she was the one who first sat with him under the stars; she taught him that "we look now" because the comet might never come back — so she would understand the importance of his discovery. (1)

14. Award 1 mark per valid impression + evidence (max 3): (3)
    Examples of acceptable impressions:
    - **Kind / gentle:** she doesn't scold him; brings him warm spiced milk.
    - **Wise / thoughtful:** *"the sky is the oldest book in the world"*; talks about what cannot be taken from you.
    - **Patient:** sits beside him on the cold tiles in the middle of the night.
    - **Loving / family-minded:** passes on what her own grandmother taught her.
    - **Encouraging of curiosity:** doesn't tell him to come down; shares the moment instead.

**Total: 25 marks**

### Marking guidance
- Accept any sensible inference with **textual support**.
- Don't penalise minor spelling slips unless meaning is unclear.
- For Q14, three *different* impressions are needed — repeating the same idea twice scores only once.
