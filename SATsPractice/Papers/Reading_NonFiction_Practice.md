## Text: *The Surprising Genius of the Octopus*

> If you were to draw up a list of the cleverest animals on Earth, you might think first of chimpanzees, dolphins, or perhaps a particularly bright dog. You would probably not think of a soft, eight-armed creature drifting in a tank. Yet many scientists now believe that the common octopus is one of the most intelligent animals in the sea — and certainly the most surprising.
>
> Octopuses (which, despite a popular myth, is the correct plural — not "octopi") have brains the size of a walnut. That does not sound impressive until you learn that they also have a further bundle of nerve cells running down **each** of their eight arms. In effect, an octopus thinks with its whole body. Each arm can taste, touch, and even make small decisions on its own, while the central brain coordinates the bigger picture.
>
> In laboratories around the world, octopuses have astonished researchers by solving puzzles that would defeat most pets. They have been filmed unscrewing the lids of jars to reach a tasty crab inside. One famous octopus in a German aquarium, called Otto, learned to squirt jets of water at the overhead lamp whenever he wanted the lights turned off — short-circuiting the entire building on more than one occasion. Staff eventually gave up and accepted that Otto simply preferred the dark.
>
> Octopuses are also masters of disguise. Special cells in their skin, called **chromatophores**, allow them to change colour in less than a second. Some species can even alter the *texture* of their skin to mimic the bumpy surface of a rock or the wrinkled fronds of seaweed. The mimic octopus of Indonesia goes further still: it can impersonate the shape and movement of at least fifteen other sea creatures, from flatfish to sea snakes, depending on which predator is nearby.
>
> Sadly, the octopus lives a short life. Most species survive for only one or two years, dying soon after they breed. This is one reason why scientists find them so fascinating: an octopus must learn everything it knows extremely quickly, without ever meeting its parents or being taught.
>
> Perhaps the strangest thing of all is how *different* an octopus is from us. Our last common ancestor lived around six hundred million years ago — a tiny, worm-like creature with no real brain at all. Intelligence, in other words, has evolved on Earth more than once, in completely separate branches of life. When we look into the slot-shaped pupil of an octopus, we are, in a small but real way, meeting an alien mind.
