## Text: *The Lighthouse Keeper's Secret*

> The wind howled across the rocky outcrop as Mira climbed the iron staircase to the lamp room. Each step rang out beneath her boots, echoing into the empty stairwell below. At the top, the great brass lantern stood silent, its prisms cold and dark. For sixty years, the Hawksmoor lighthouse had warned ships away from the treacherous reef known locally as the Devil's Teeth. But tonight, for the first time anyone could remember, the light had failed.
>
> Mira's grandfather had been the keeper here until his retirement. As a small girl, she had spent summers with him, learning the rhythm of the tides, the names of the birds, and — above all — the importance of the lamp. "The light," he would say, his weathered hand resting on the brass casing, "is a promise. Sailors trust it with their lives." She had nodded solemnly, not yet understanding what such a promise might cost.
>
> Now, with a storm rolling in from the west and a fishing fleet due back at any moment, the promise had been broken. Mira knelt by the mechanism and shone her torch into the gears. A single, neatly-folded piece of paper was wedged between two cogs. Frowning, she eased it out.
>
> The handwriting was unmistakably her grandfather's.
>
> *"If you are reading this, the lamp has stopped — and you, my brave one, have come. Look beneath the third floorboard from the door."*
>
> Outside, the first lash of rain struck the windows like gunfire. Mira hurried down the spiral steps, her heart thudding. The third floorboard lifted easily. Beneath it lay a small, tarnished key and a logbook bound in cracked leather. The key fitted the cabinet behind the workbench — a cabinet she had been told, all her life, was simply broken and could not be opened. Inside, dust-covered but in perfect order, sat a second lamp mechanism, its brass gleaming under her torch. A note was taped to the casing.
>
> *"For the night the first one fails. Trust your training. — Grandad"*
>
> Mira allowed herself one small smile, then set to work. The fleet was coming home, and the promise still had to be kept.
