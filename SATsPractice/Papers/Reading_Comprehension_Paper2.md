## Text: *The Boy Who Watched the Sky*

> The first time Arun saw the comet, he was eleven years old and lying flat on the cool tiles of his grandmother's roof terrace in Jaipur. The city below had finally fallen quiet. Above him, the stars wheeled in their slow, ancient pattern — and then, low in the east, a smudge of silver light appeared that did not belong to any star map he had memorised.
>
> He stared. The smudge had a faint, feathery tail. It was, he realised with a jolt, moving.
>
> Arun's grandmother, Nani, found him still on the roof at half past two in the morning, his neck stiff, his eyes shining. She did not scold him. Instead, she fetched two cups of warm spiced milk and sat beside him on the tiles.
>
> "When I was a girl," she said quietly, "my own grandmother taught me the names of the stars. She said the sky is the oldest book in the world, and the only one no one can take from you. You can lose every other thing, Arun — every house, every coin, every keepsake — but you cannot lose the sky."
>
> Arun did not answer at once. He was thinking. The comet hung above the rooftops like a stranger who had come a very long way to say hello.
>
> "Will it come back?" he asked at last.
>
> Nani smiled. "Perhaps in seventy years. Perhaps never. That is why we look now."
>
> ---
>
> Twenty-three years later, Dr Arun Mehra sat in a small, brightly-lit office in California, scrolling through data that no human eye had ever seen before. On his screen, three faint pixels — picked out by the orbiting telescope his team had built — formed a pattern. A slow, careful pattern. Something out beyond Neptune was moving in a way the computer could not explain.
>
> His colleagues teased him gently for the photograph on his desk: a creased picture of a small boy on a rooftop, a steel cup of milk in one hand, his other hand pointing upwards. Arun did not mind the teasing. He had learned, long ago, that the most important discoveries do not begin in laboratories. They begin in small, dark places where someone chooses to look up instead of looking away.
>
> He leaned closer to the screen. The three pixels shifted again. Whatever was out there, it had not been seen before — and tonight, in a quiet office on the other side of the world from a rooftop in Jaipur, it had been seen at last.
>
> Arun reached for his phone. There was, he thought, only one person he wanted to call first. He hoped Nani was still awake.
